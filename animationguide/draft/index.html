How To Animate A Scene

(Illustrations to come during final draft)

This guide is for digital animation and assumes the possession of a drawing tablet.
The examples are made in Adobe Animate but this guide is applicable to any animation software.
Basic knowledge of your animation software of choice is assumed for this guide.




Part 1: Storyboards

Plan out the animation

To start, you will make a storyboard. Every second or so, create a "keyframe".
This is a frame that happens between major movements during the animation.
Draw any characters and moving objects during the scene for the keyframes.
Also sketch out the background during this step, so you know where in the frame to draw your characters.

Tips & Tricks

Make sure to have a script and voice lines ready before you start the animation process.
You don't want to redo animation just because the script needed a change.
---
You will eventually want to get a reference sheet for your characters.
This is a sheet that has the character drawn with correct proportions on it to ensure consistency.
It isn't incredibly important for a beginner; just try to keep your characters consistently sized!.
---
Keep the keyframes quick and sketchy during this step. You will draw over them in a later step.




Part 2: Line Art

Create rough animation

During this step, you will draw between the keyframes that you made in the last step.
You will be "Connecting the dots" in a way, making the movement that happens between the keyframes.
Keep these inbetween frames quick and sketchy. You will go over them again in the next step.
Also sketch out the background during this step, so you know where in the frame to draw your characters.

Draw cleanly over the rough animation

This is the longest and most time consuming step. Here, you will be tracing over your rough sketches.
Make sure that you are drawing cleanly and on a different layer than your sketches.
Start with the keyframes that you made in step one, then do the others in your preferred order..
This step can get a little repetitive and mindless, but it can also be relaxing!.

Tips & Tricks

The standard for animation in most studios is 24 frames per second. For a beginner, I'd reccomend 12.
The more frames per second, the smoother the animation is, but this comes at the cost of more effort.
Double the FPS means double the smoothness, but you'll be drawing twice the frames. Diminishing returns apply!
---
If a character has long hair or jewlery, don't draw them while sketching the rough body movements.
Go over all the frames a second time when you finish the rough animation to keep their movements seperate.
---
Make heavy use of the "onion skin" feature of your animation software while sketching.
---
All body movement happens in "arcs". If you are just drawing frames linearly between keyframes, it will look ugly.
If an arm moves, keep in mind that it has a bone and is on a joint. Same with the legs and head.




Part 3: Coloring

Color with solid colors

This is the most satisfying step. Color in your characters and objects with solid colors.
Don't worry about shading, that will be done after the initial colors.
The background can be colored at any time, but I reccomend doing it at the start of
this step to allow you to plan the object and character colors around the background.

Shade the scene

For the final step, start shading each onject in the scene.
For every color in every frame, imagine where in the scene the light is coming from.
Shade the side of every object in every frame where a light is not facing.
For instance, if it is high noon, shade the bottom of the objects. For dark places with a
single light source, shade the sides that are facing away from the light.

Tips & Tricks

There is no shame in using the paint bucket tool to instantly fill in an area.
For open line art where you can't use the paint bucket tool, use the "Draw Inside" tool
to "plaster over" the hole in the line art and then use the paint bucket tool.
---

Most animation software has a feature that allows you to draw only in the first color that you
start drawing in. This is usually called "Draw Inside" and it is very good for shading.
---
While shading with the color yellow, never just shade with darker yellow.
Always either go darker with a little bit of red or blue added to avoid dark yellow, which is ugly.
